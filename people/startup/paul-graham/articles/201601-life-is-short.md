# Life is Short

Life is short, as everyone knows. When I was a kid I used to wonder about this. Is life actually short, or are we really complaining about its finiteness? Would we be just as likely to feel life was short if we lived 10 times as long?

Since there didn't seem any way to answer this question, I stopped wondering about it. Then I had kids. That gave me a way to answer the question, and the answer is that **life actually is short**.

## Converting Time to Discrete Quantities

Having kids showed me how to convert a continuous quantity, time, into discrete quantities. **You only get 52 weekends with your 2 year old.** If Christmas-as-magic lasts from say ages 3 to 10, you only get to watch your child experience it 8 times. And while it's impossible to say what is a lot or a little of a continuous quantity like time, 8 is not a lot of something. If you had a handful of 8 peanuts, or a shelf of 8 books to choose from, the quantity would definitely seem limited, no matter what your lifespan was.

## The Real Problem: Wasted Time

Ok, so life actually is short. Does it make any difference to know that?

It has for me. It means arguments of the form "Life is too short for x" have great force. It's not just a figure of speech to say that life is too short for something. It's not just a synonym for annoying. If you find yourself thinking that life is too short for something, you should try to eliminate it if you can.

### What to Eliminate

When I ask myself what I've found life is too short for, the word that pops into my head is **"bullshit."** I realize that answer is somewhat tautological. It's almost the definition of bullshit that it's the stuff that life is too short for. And yet bullshit does have a distinctive character. There's something fake about it.

**Two types of things that feel like bullshit**:
1. Things that are genuinely fake or dishonest
2. Things that are merely tedious

It's dangerous to use the word "bullshit" to mean both, because when you think something is bullshit because it's tedious, you're also implying it's fake. And that's often not true.

## Pruning Your Life

**The most dangerous way to lose time is not to spend it having fun, but to spend it doing fake work.** When you spend time having fun, you know you're being self-indulgent. But the other way time gets used up is by errands that appear necessary but aren't.

The way to fight this is to:
1. **Identify what matters most to you**
2. **Be ruthlessly honest about what's actually important**
3. **Say no to almost everything else**

## Relationships and Time

Relentlessly prune bullshit, don't wait to do things that matter, and **savor the time you have**. That's what you do when life is short.

One thing that falls into the category of things we put off is spending time with people we care about. We imagine we'll have plenty of time for that later. But life is short. If you want to spend time with someone, do it now.

## The Urgency of Now

The usual way to avoid being explicit about what you dislike is to use euphemisms. But life is too short for euphemisms. If you're in a relationship with someone who's making you unhappy, get out now. There's no point waiting.

Similarly, if there's something you want to do, do it now. Don't wait. Life is short.

## Against Busy-ness

Another thing I've noticed is that **busy-ness is a defense mechanism**. People who are chronically busy are usually avoiding something. Often they're avoiding the fact that what they're doing doesn't matter.

When you're busy, you're productive. Doing things. You feel virtuous. But being busy isn't a virtue. What matters is what you're busy doing.

## The Taste of Time

One of the most striking things I've learned is that the taste of life is better when you have less time. The things that taste good when you have all the time in the world to do them don't taste as good when you have limited time. Scarcity makes you more selective, and being more selective means you do better things.

---

**Core Takeaway**: Life is genuinely short, not just finite. This means you should ruthlessly eliminate bullshit, spend time on what matters, and especially spend time with people you care about—now, not later.
