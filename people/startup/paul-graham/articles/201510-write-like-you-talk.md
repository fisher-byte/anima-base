# Write Like You Talk

Here's a simple trick for getting more people to read what you write: **write in spoken language**.

## The Writing Transformation

Something comes over most people when they start writing. They write in a different language than they'd use if they were talking to a friend. The sentence structure and even the words are different. No one uses "pen" as a verb in spoken English. You'd feel like an idiot using "pen" instead of "write" in a conversation with a friend.

### Example of Formal Writing

The last straw for me was a sentence I read a couple days ago:

> "The mercurial Spaniard himself declared: 'After Altamira, all is decadence.'"

Just imagine calling Picasso "the mercurial Spaniard" when talking to a friend. Even one sentence of this would raise eyebrows in conversation. And yet people write whole books of it.

## Is Written Language Better?

Ok, so written and spoken language are different. Does that make written language worse?

**If you want people to read and understand what you write, yes.** Written language is:
- More complex (more work to read)
- More formal and distant (gives reader's attention permission to drift)
- Gives false impression you're saying more than you are

## Complex Ideas Don't Need Complex Sentences

**You don't need complex sentences to express complex ideas.** When specialists in some abstruse topic talk to one another about ideas in their field, they don't use sentences any more complex than they do when talking about what to have for lunch.

They use different words, certainly. But even those they use no more than necessary. And in my experience, **the harder the subject, the more informally experts speak**. Partly, I think, because:
1. They have less to prove
2. The harder the ideas, the less you can afford to let language get in the way

**Informal language is the athletic clothing of ideas.**

## When Formal Writing Works

I'm not saying spoken language always works best:
- **Poetry**: As much music as text, can say things you wouldn't say in conversation
- **A handful of great writers**: Can get away with fancy language in prose
- **Corporate bad news**: Writers don't want to make it easy to understand
- **Bogus humanities**: Deliberately obscure

But for nearly everyone else, spoken language is better.

## How to Write Like You Talk

It seems to be hard for most people to write in spoken language. So perhaps the best solution is:

1. **Write your first draft normally**
2. **Look at each sentence and ask**: "Is this the way I'd say this if I were talking to a friend?"
3. **If not**: Imagine what you would say, and use that instead
4. **After a while**: This filter will start to operate as you write

## My Process

Before I publish a new essay, I **read it out loud** and fix everything that doesn't sound like conversation. I even fix bits that are phonetically awkward; I don't know if that's necessary, but it doesn't cost much.

### For Severe Cases

This trick may not always be enough. I've seen writing so far removed from spoken language that it couldn't be fixed sentence by sentence. For cases like that there's a more drastic solution:

**After writing the first draft, try explaining to a friend what you just wrote. Then replace the draft with what you said to your friend.**

## The Sound of Conversation

People often tell me how much my essays sound like me talking. The fact that this seems worthy of comment shows how rarely people manage to write in spoken language. Otherwise everyone's writing would sound like them talking.

**If you simply manage to write in spoken language, you'll be ahead of 95% of writers.** And it's so easy to do: just don't let a sentence through unless it's the way you'd say it to a friend.

---

**Core Takeaway**: Writing in spoken language makes your ideas clearer and more engaging. Most people write in a more complex, formal style than they speak—and their writing suffers for it. The solution: read your writing out loud and fix anything that doesn't sound like conversation.
