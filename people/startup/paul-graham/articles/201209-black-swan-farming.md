# Black Swan Farming

I've done several types of work over the years but I don't know another as counterintuitive as startup investing.

## Two Counterintuitive Rules

The two most important things to understand about startup investing, as a business, are:

1. **Effectively all the returns are concentrated in a few big winners**
2. **The best ideas look initially like bad ideas**

### Rule 1: Power Law Returns

I knew the first rule intellectually, but didn't really grasp it till it happened to us. The total value of the companies we've funded is around 10 billion, give or take a few. But just two companies, **Dropbox and Airbnb, account for about three quarters of it**.

In startups, the big winners are big to a degree that violates our expectations about variation. The distribution of outcomes is not a normal distribution—it's a power law. The biggest successes are not 10x bigger than the median, but 1000x or more.

### Rule 2: Bad Ideas That Aren't

The second rule is even more counterintuitive. The best startup ideas look wrong initially. If they obviously looked good, someone would already be doing them.

This is part of what makes startup investing so difficult:
- You have to fund things that look bad
- But not actually bad things
- Just things that look bad to other people

## Implications for Investing

These two rules have profound implications for how to do startup investing:

### You Must Be in the Game
Since returns are concentrated in a few big winners, **you can't afford to miss the big winners**. Missing one Airbnb or Dropbox costs you years of returns.

This means:
- You need to make enough investments to get some winners
- You can't be too selective—you might filter out the winners
- You need to fund founders even when you're not sure

### You Must Take Chances on Unusual Ideas
Since the best ideas look bad initially, you have to be willing to fund ideas that seem wrong. This requires:
- Ignoring conventional wisdom
- Trusting founders more than the idea
- Being comfortable looking foolish

## The Farming Metaphor

The metaphor I use is farming, specifically farming for black swans (extremely rare, high-impact events):

**Traditional farming**: Plant many seeds, most grow predictably, get consistent yields

**Black swan farming**: Plant many seeds, most die, but a few become enormous trees that dwarf everything else combined

Startup investing is black swan farming. You're not trying to get average returns from all your investments. You're trying to get exceptional returns from a few, which will more than compensate for the losers.

## Why This Is Hard

This approach is psychologically difficult:
- Most of your investments will fail or return little
- You'll look foolish funding many "bad" ideas
- You need patience—big winners take time
- You can't stop too early or you'll miss the big ones

But it's the only approach that works, given the power law distribution of returns.

## The Batch Model

At Y Combinator, we address this by funding batches of startups:
- Fund many startups at once (typically 50-100 per batch)
- Know that most will fail
- But some will be huge
- The winners pay for everything else and more

This isn't just accepting failure—it's **a strategy built around the power law**. We're not trying to avoid failure; we're trying to get into the game where the winners are big enough to matter.

## Changes Over Time

One thing that's changed since 2012: raising money after Demo Day is no longer as critical. The ecosystem has evolved, and companies have more options and time.

---

**Core Takeaway**: Startup investing follows a power law distribution where a tiny number of huge winners generate almost all returns. This requires investors to make many bets on ideas that initially look bad, knowing most will fail but a few will be extraordinary.
