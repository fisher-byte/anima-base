# The Refragmentation

One advantage of being old is that you can see change happen in your lifetime. A lot of the change I've seen is **fragmentation**:
- US politics is much more polarized than it used to be
- Culturally we have ever less common ground
- The creative class flocks to a handful of happy cities, abandoning the rest
- Increasing economic inequality means the spread between rich and poor is growing too

## The Hypothesis

I'd like to propose a hypothesis: that **all these trends are instances of the same phenomenon**. And moreover, that the cause is not some force that's pulling us apart, but rather the erosion of forces that had been pushing us together.

## The 20th Century: Artificial Togetherness

The mid-20th century had unusually high unity:
- Three TV networks everyone watched
- Major newspapers everyone read
- Large corporations everyone worked for
- Standardized education
- Mass production, mass media, mass culture

**But this wasn't the natural state of things.** It was an aberration caused by:
1. **World Wars**: Created national unity and large organizations
2. **Technology of the era**: Favored centralization and mass production
3. **Regulation**: Prevented fragmentation in many industries

## The Return to Normal

What we're seeing now isn't fragmentation—it's **refragmentation**. We're returning to a more natural state that existed before the industrial era temporarily pushed everyone together.

### Historical Precedent

Before the 20th century:
- Media was fragmented (many local newspapers)
- Work was fragmented (small businesses, craftsmen)
- Culture was fragmented (regional differences)
- Education was fragmented (many approaches)

## Why Refragmentation Is Happening

### 1. Technology Changed
The technologies that favored centralization (broadcast, mass production) are being replaced by technologies that favor fragmentation (internet, digital production).

### 2. Regulation Changed
Deregulation in many industries allowed more competition and variety.

### 3. The Wars Ended
The unifying effect of World Wars wore off over generations.

## Is This Good or Bad?

**It's neither—it's just different.** But it has implications:

### Downsides
- Less social cohesion
- More echo chambers
- Harder to coordinate on large problems
- More inequality (winner-take-most in many domains)

### Upsides
- More choice and customization
- Better matching (people, jobs, places)
- More innovation and experimentation
- More meritocracy (less dependence on gatekeepers)

## Implications for Individuals

In a refragmented world:

**Find your niche**: Instead of trying to appeal to everyone, find the specific people who care about what you care about.

**Build networks**: Your network becomes more important when there's less institutional glue holding things together.

**Be more selective**: You have more choices, which means choosing wisely matters more.

**Embrace change**: The pace of fragmentation will likely accelerate, not slow down.

## Implications for Society

We need to develop new ways to:
- Create shared understanding across fragments
- Coordinate on collective challenges
- Maintain social bonds without artificial unity
- Balance freedom with cohesion

## The Technology Driver

The most important driver of refragmentation is **technology**. Specifically:
- Internet → fragmented media
- Software → fragmented work
- Digital distribution → fragmented culture
- Online communities → fragmented social life

**This trend will continue** because technology continues to make fragmentation more feasible and desirable.

## Looking Forward

The 20th century's unity was temporary. The refragmentation we're experiencing is likely permanent, or at least very long-lasting.

**The question isn't whether to stop it** (we can't), but **how to adapt**. Both individuals and institutions need to figure out how to thrive in a more fragmented world.

---

**Core Takeaway**: Modern polarization and fragmentation isn't a new force pulling us apart—it's the erosion of 20th-century forces that temporarily pushed us together. We're returning to a more natural, fragmented state driven by technology and social change.
