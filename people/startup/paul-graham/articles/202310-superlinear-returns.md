# Superlinear Returns

One of the most important things I didn't understand about the world when I was a child is the degree to which the returns for performance are superlinear.

Teachers and coaches implicitly told us the returns were linear. "You get out," I heard a thousand times, "what you put in." They meant well, but this is rarely true. If your product is only half as good as your competitor's, you don't get half as many customers. You get no customers, and you go out of business.

## The Nature of Superlinear Returns

It's obviously true that the returns for performance are superlinear in business. Some think this is a flaw of capitalism, and that if we changed the rules it would stop being true. But superlinear returns are a feature of the world, not an artifact of rules we've invented. We see the same pattern in many natural systems.

**Key insight**: Superlinear returns appear whenever you have:
- Learning (knowledge builds on itself)
- Network effects (value increases with more participants)
- Thresholds (minimum viable performance levels)
- Competition (winner-take-most dynamics)

## Where Superlinear Returns Appear

### 1. Learning and Knowledge
When you learn something, it makes learning related things easier. Knowledge compounds. This is why the most successful people are often those who've accumulated the most knowledge in their field.

### 2. Performance and Reputation
Your reputation grows superlinearly with performance. Being twice as good doesn't just get you twice the opportunities—it gets you dramatically more, because people want to work with the best.

### 3. Exponential Growth
Many systems exhibit exponential growth:
- Money compounds through investment
- Networks grow through word of mouth
- Skills compound through practice

### 4. Winner-Take-Most Markets
In many domains, being slightly better than competitors doesn't yield slightly more success—it yields dramatically more. The best restaurant in town gets far more than its proportional share of diners.

## Implications for Work and Life

**Choose carefully what to work on**: Since returns are superlinear, it's crucial to work on things where small improvements in quality yield large improvements in results.

**Aim for excellence, not adequacy**: In a linear world, being merely adequate might make sense. In a superlinear world, the difference between good and great is enormous.

**Be patient**: Superlinear returns often take time to materialize. Early on, improvements may seem insignificant. But they compound.

**Work on things you're naturally good at**: Your comparative advantage is amplified in domains with superlinear returns.

## The Compounding Effect

The most powerful form of superlinear returns comes from compounding—when the outputs of one period become the inputs of the next:
- Learning: each thing you learn makes future learning easier
- Money: investment returns get reinvested
- Reputation: success attracts more opportunities
- Network: each connection opens doors to more connections

## Following Your Curiosity

You don't need to deliberately seek out domains with superlinear returns. If you have sufficient natural ability and you follow your curiosity sufficiently far, you'll end up in one. Your curiosity won't let you be interested in boring questions, and interesting questions tend to create superlinear returns.

---

**Core Takeaway**: Understanding that returns are superlinear—not linear—is fundamental to making good decisions about what to work on, how to work, and how to think about success. Small differences in performance often lead to dramatically different outcomes.
