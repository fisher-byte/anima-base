# Obviously Awesome: How to Position Your Product with April Dunford

## Overview

April Dunford discusses her book "Obviously Awesome" and shares insights on product positioning with host Brian Rhea on the Bright & Early podcast. The conversation covers the positioning methodology, common pitfalls, and practical examples from her 25+ years of experience in tech startups.

## Key Insights

### 1. **Positioning is Often Misunderstood**

Most companies have positioning, but it's not deliberate or well-thought-out. The result is:
- Misalignment between how companies describe their products and how customers understand them
- Weak positioning that fails to differentiate
- Sales teams struggling to articulate value

### 2. **The Five Components of Positioning**

April breaks down positioning into five interconnected elements:

1. **Competitive Alternatives**: What would customers use if your product didn't exist?
2. **Differentiated Capabilities**: What makes your product unique?
3. **Differentiated Value**: Why should customers care about those unique features?
4. **Target Customer Segments**: Who cares the most about that value?
5. **Market Category**: What context helps customers understand your value?

### 3. **Market Category is Critical**

The market category you choose:
- Sets expectations for how your product should work
- Defines who you compete against
- Influences pricing assumptions
- Shapes the buying process

**Example**: Positioning a product as "CRM" vs "CRM for investment banks" dramatically changes customer expectations and competitive landscape.

### 4. **Start with Differentiation, Not Market**

Most companies make the mistake of:
- Starting with a broad market category (e.g., "We're a CRM")
- Then trying to explain what makes them different

Better approach:
- Identify your unique differentiated capabilities first
- Find the market context that best showcases those capabilities
- Choose the category that makes your strengths obvious

### 5. **The Calendar Example**

April shares a memorable analogy:
- If a CRM doesn't have a calendar, customers in the traditional CRM market will say "You suck"
- But if you position it as "CRM for investment banking," the lack of calendar becomes irrelevant
- The market category defines which features matter

## Positioning Methodology

### Step 1: List Your Differentiated Capabilities
What can your product do that alternatives cannot?

### Step 2: Map to Value
For each capability, ask: "So what? Why does this matter?"

### Step 3: Identify Best-Fit Customers
Who cares most deeply about this specific value?

### Step 4: Define the Market Category
What category makes your differentiation most obvious?

### Step 5: Test and Iterate
Position changes should be tested with:
- Sales teams (how does it change conversations?)
- Customers (does it resonate?)
- Market (does it stand out?)

## Common Positioning Mistakes

### Mistake #1: Following Competitors
"If everyone calls themselves X, we should too" leads to commoditization

### Mistake #2: Being Too Broad
Trying to appeal to everyone dilutes your message and wastes resources

### Mistake #3: Feature-Focused Positioning
Listing features without connecting to customer outcomes

### Mistake #4: Ignoring Competitive Alternatives
Not understanding what customers would use instead of your product

## Practical Applications

### For Startups
- Position early and deliberately
- Don't default to the biggest market category
- Find the niche where you can dominate

### For Sales Teams
- Use positioning to qualify leads faster
- Frame demos around differentiated value
- Handle objections by reframing the competitive set

### For Product Teams
- Prioritize features that strengthen differentiation
- Say no to features that muddy positioning
- Build for your best-fit customers first

## Key Quotes

> "Most companies have positioning, but it's accidental, not deliberate."

> "The market category you choose sets the context for everything—features, pricing, competitors."

> "You can't position in a vacuum. You have to position against alternatives."

> "Weak positioning is one of the biggest reasons products fail, even when the product itself is great."

## Takeaways for Product Leaders

1. **Positioning is Strategic Work**: It's not just marketing—it impacts product, sales, and customer success
2. **Be Specific**: Narrow positioning often leads to broader success
3. **Test with Customers**: The best positioning resonates with your best customers
4. **Align the Team**: Sales, marketing, and product must share the same positioning
5. **Revisit Regularly**: As your product and market evolve, so should your positioning
