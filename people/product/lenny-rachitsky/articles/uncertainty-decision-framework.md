# Navigating Uncertainty Decision Framework

> Uncertainty never goes away. You just learn to live with it. Waiting for "more certainty" is not a strategy.

## Overview

**Jeremy Henrickson's Core Insight**: In fast-scaling environments (Coinbase, Rippling), uncertainty is constant.

**Key experiences**:
- Crypto extreme volatility
- COVID-19 sudden arrival
- Geopolitical unexpected shifts
- Competitive landscape rapid evolution

**Conclusion**: Learning to make decisions under uncertainty is a core skill, not optional.

---

## Decision Classification Framework

### The 2x2 Matrix

**Jeremy's framework**: Not all decisions are equally important.

```
                    High Importance          Low Importance
Reversible decisions   Quick decision            Quick decision
                    (low risk)               (low risk)

Irreversible         Deep thinking            Quick decision
decisions            (can delegate)           (can delegate)
                    (high importance)         (may delegate)
```

### Decision Rules by Type

| Type | Characteristics | Action | Examples |
|-------|----------------|--------|----------|
| **Reversible + Low Importance** | Act immediately, don't analyze | Small feature flag, copy change, meeting time |
| **Reversible + High Importance** | Quick action, track results | Pricing test, UX change, marketing campaign |
| **Irreversible + High Importance** | Deep thinking, consult experts | Hiring C-level, strategic pivot, major partnership |
| **Irreversible + Low Importance** | Delegate or automate | Routine tasks, documentation, reporting |

**Key insight**: Most decisions are reversible. Don't treat all decisions like they're irreversible.

## Principle 2: Minimize Decision Cost

### The Cost of Indecision Problem

**Jeremy's observation**: Most teams spend too much time on the decision itself, not the decision.

**Common issues**:
- Over-analyzing, waiting for perfect information (never comes)
- Seeking consensus from everyone (impossible, also unnecessary)
- Fear of making wrong decision (leads to no decision)

**Jeremy's experience from Coinbase**:
> "At Coinbase, we made some wrong decisions. But we corrected them. We never died from not deciding. We died from not deciding fast enough."

### The Decision Cost Framework

**Solution components**:

**1. Set Time Limits**
- "We have 1 hour to decide X"
- "We'll discuss this for 2 meetings maximum"
- "Decision by Friday EOD, moving forward either way"

**2. Use Decision Frameworks**
- RICE for prioritization
- Confidence meter for uncertain decisions
- PMF 40% rule for product decisions

**3. Accept Decisions Are Adjusttable**
- Make decision
- Track outcomes
- Adjust if needed
- Don't fear revision

**Decision cost equation**:
```
Total cost = (Time to decide) + (Cost of wrong decision)
            - (Cost of delay)
```

**Lenny's insight**: Delay often costs more than being wrong (missed opportunity, market passes by, competitors move faster).

## Principle 3: Decision-Maker vs. Consult

### Role Clarity Framework

**Problem**: Decisions stall because responsibility is unclear.

**Solution**: Explicitly define roles.

**Decision-maker**:
- Has decision authority
- Accountable for outcome
- Can be CEO, product owner, PM

**Consultant**:
- Has domain expertise
- Provides data and insights
- Doesn't make final decision

### Rippling's Method

**Explicitly assign**:
- Decision-maker for each decision domain
- Decision rules (when to escalate, what can be decided autonomously)
- Consultation requirements (who must be consulted, who's informed)

**Benefits**:
- Clear accountability
- Faster decisions (no circular debates)
- Empowerment (team can act within authority)
- Learning (clear who to learn from)

### When to Use This Framework

**Apply when**:
- Organization growing (new decision points)
- Cross-functional decisions (marketing + product + engineering)
- High-stakes decisions (strategy, partnerships)
- Ambiguity in who decides

**Decision checklist**:
1. Who is decision-maker?
2. Who must be consulted?
3. Who should be informed?
4. What's the decision deadline?
5. What's the decision criteria?

## Speed vs. Quality Trade-off

### The "Good Enough" Decision Framework

**Jeremy's insight**: In uncertainty, good enough > perfect.

**When to prioritize speed**:
- Market moving quickly (competitors launching)
- Customer feedback urgent (users frustrated)
- Low-information decisions (data not available anyway)
- Reversible decisions (can iterate)

**When to prioritize quality**:
- High-stakes, irreversible decisions
- Safety, compliance, legal matters
- Significant resource commitment (hiring, major contracts)
- Long-term strategic bets

**Decision flow**:

```
Decision arises
    ↓
[Classification: Reversible or Irreversible?]
    ↓ Reversible → Quick decision (70% certainty)
    ↓ Irreversible
[Classification: High or Low Importance?]
    ↓ Low → Delegate or automate
    ↓ High
[Classification: How much information available?]
    ↓ High → Deep thinking, consult experts
    ↓ Low
[Decision: Good enough or perfect?]
    → Good enough (decide now, iterate)
    → Perfect (research, deliberate)
```

## Decision Under Uncertainty: Practical Tactics

### Tactic 1: Time-Box Every Decision

**Rule**: Set decision deadline before discussion starts.

**Examples**:
- "We'll spend 2 hours on this, then decide"
- "Feedback by Friday, decision next Monday"
- "Test for 4 weeks, then commit or kill"

**Why it works**:
- Forces prioritization (what's most important to figure out?)
- Prevents endless analysis
- Creates urgency
- Enables progress

### Tactic 2: The 70% Certainty Rule

**From Annie Duke (via Lenny)**

**Rule**: Make decisions with 70% certainty, not 100%.

**Why**:
- 100% certainty rarely comes
- Last 20% of certainty often costs 80% of time
- You can iterate after decision

**Application**:
- Gather 70% of available information
- Make decision
- Track outcomes
- Learn and adjust

### Tactic 3: Pre-Mortem Decisions

**From Shreyas Doshi (via Lenny)**

**Rule**: Before implementing, imagine it failed.

**Ask**:
- What could go wrong?
- What assumptions might be false?
- What market conditions might change?
- How would we respond?

**Benefits**:
- Uncovers risks early
- Creates contingency plans
- Reduces surprise impact
- Improves decision quality

### Tactic 4: Decision Tracking

**Jeremy's practice**: Track decisions and outcomes.

**Decision journal format**:
1. What was decision?
2. What information did we have?
3. What options did we consider?
4. What was our rationale?
5. What outcome did we observe?
6. What would we do differently?

**Review periodicaly** (quarterly):
- What decisions were right?
- What decisions were wrong?
- What patterns emerge?
- What can we improve?

## Common Uncertainty Pitfalls

### ❌ Paralysis by Analysis

**Problem**: Overthinking, waiting for perfect information

**Reality**: Perfect information never arrives in uncertainty.

**Fix**:
- Set time limits
- Decide with 70% certainty
- Accept you'll iterate

### ❌ Consensus Seeking

**Problem**: Waiting for everyone to agree

**Reality**: Agreement on everything is impossible and unnecessary.

**Fix**:
- Gather input (consultants)
- Make decision (decision-maker)
- Communicate rationale (explain why)

### ❌ Fear of Being Wrong

**Problem**: Not deciding to avoid mistakes

**Reality**: Not deciding is usually worse than being wrong.

**Jeremy's insight**:
> "We made wrong decisions at Coinbase. We corrected them. We never died from wrong decisions—we died from not deciding fast enough."

**Fix**:
- Accept decisions are reversible (mostly)
- Track and adjust
- Learn from mistakes

### ❌ Role Ambiguity

**Problem**: Unclear who decides

**Reality**: Decisions stall, accountability unclear.

**Fix**:
- Explicitly assign decision-maker
- Define decision rules
- Use consult vs. decide framework

## Case Example: Scaling Under Uncertainty

### Scenario: Product Strategy Decision

**Context**: Growing startup, market shifting rapidly

**Decision**: Should we pivot to new market segment?

**Framework application**:

1. **Classification**: Reversible? Yes (can return if fails)
2. **Importance**: High (major strategic direction)
3. **Information availability**: Low (uncertain market demand)

**Decision**:
- Time-box: 1 week to research and decide
- Consult: Talk to 10 potential customers
- Decision rule: If 4+ express strong interest → pivot

**Outcome tracking**:
- Decision made
- 6 months later: Results positive (growing faster)
- Review: What worked? What didn't? What would we do differently?
