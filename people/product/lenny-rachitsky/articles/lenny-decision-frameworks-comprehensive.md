# Lenny's Favorite Decision-Making Frameworks

> Companies live and die by the quality of their decisions. One better decision can change the trajectory of your business. Yet important decisions often get mired in head-banging meetings, debates, and consensus-seeking.

## Why Decision Frameworks Matter

As your company grows, you'll inevitably run into decision-making challenges. These frameworks are designed to help you make better decisions more quickly and with less drama.

**Key benefits**:
- Speed up decision-making
- Reduce conflict and drama
- Ensure alignment on outcomes
- Make trade-offs explicit
- Capture decision rationale

## The Frameworks

### 1. The 3-2-1 Decision Framework

**When to use**: Making product decisions with multiple stakeholders and options

**How it works**:
1. **3 options**: Always present at least 3 distinct options for any decision
2. **2 pros**: For each option, identify at least 2 pros
3. **1 con**: For each option, identify at least 1 con
4. **Decision rule**: Choose the option with the best balance of pros/cons

**Why it works**:
- Forces consideration of alternatives
- Prevents "my way or the highway" mentality
- Makes trade-offs visible
- Reduces bias toward first idea

**Example**: Choosing a new feature direction
- Option A: Build feature X
  - Pro: Fast to ship, solves immediate user request
  - Pro: Low technical risk
  - Con: Limited differentiation
- Option B: Refactor underlying system
  - Pro: Enables many future features
  - Pro: Technical debt reduction
  - Con: Longer time to value
- Option C: Partner with third-party solution
  - Pro: Immediate capability
  - Pro: External innovation
  - Con: Long-term dependency risk

### 2. The Decision Owner Framework

**When to use**: Projects with multiple contributors where accountability is unclear

**Key roles**:
- **Approver**: Person with final decision authority (ONE person per domain)
- **Accountable**: Person who owns project outcome (ONE person total)
- **Actionable**: People doing the work (can be multiple)

**Rules**:
- Always have ONE accountable person per project
- Only ONE approver per decision domain
- Make roles visible to everyone
- Reference roles when questions arise

**See also**: [AAA Framework - Shopify](/people/product/lenny-rachitsky/twitter/aaa-framework-shopify.md)

### 3. The Confidence Meter Framework

**When to use**: Prioritizing ideas when resources are limited

**How it works**:
For each idea/product opportunity, rate confidence on 1-10 scale:
- 1-3: Low confidence - don't pursue yet
- 4-6: Medium confidence - consider with caution
- 7-8: High confidence - prioritize
- 9-10: Very high confidence - top priority

**Factors that boost confidence**:
- User validation (interviews, surveys, usage data)
- Clear problem definition
- Obvious demand signals
- Team execution capability
- Market trend alignment

**Factors that reduce confidence**:
- Assumed user needs
- Unclear value proposition
- Competitor saturation
- Resource constraints
- High technical risk

**See also**: [Evidence-Guided Product Development - Itamar Gilad](/people/product/lenny-rachitsky/podcasts/2023-09-21-itamar-gilad.md)

### 4. The Time-Boxing Framework

**When to use**: Decisions that could drag on endlessly

**How it works**:
1. Set a decision deadline before starting discussion
2. Allocate time proportional to decision importance
3. At deadline, make the best decision possible with available information
4. Move forward—don't re-open without new information

**Time allocation guide**:
- Low impact decisions: 15-30 min
- Medium impact decisions: 1-2 hours or 1 meeting
- High impact decisions: 1-2 days or 2-3 meetings
- Critical decisions: 1 week maximum

**Key insight**: A good decision made quickly beats a perfect decision made never.

### 5. The Stakeholder Alignment Framework

**When to use**: Decisions affecting multiple teams/functions

**Process**:
1. **Identify stakeholders**: Who will be impacted? Who has relevant expertise?
2. **Gather input**: Seek their perspectives individually or in small groups
3. **Check for blockers**: Does anyone have a "veto" concern?
4. **Address blockers**: Understand and resolve blocking issues
5. **Make decision**: Decision-maker decides with all input
6. **Communicate**: Explain decision and rationale to all stakeholders

**Common mistake**: Seeking consensus vs. seeking input.
- ✅ Right: Gather input, then decide
- ❌ Wrong: Wait until everyone agrees (rarely happens)

### 6. The Two-Door Test

**From Shreyas Doshi**

**When to use**: Evaluating major decisions or pivots

**How it works**:
Ask: "If we go through this door, is there a way to come back through another door?"

- **Two-door decisions**: You can try and reverse (low risk)
- **One-door decisions**: Committing without easy exit (high risk)

**Application**:
- Use two-door approach for experiments, feature launches, market tests
- Use one-door approach carefully for infrastructure, partnerships, team commitments

**Caution**: Be skeptical of "quick two-door" framing—sometimes important decisions require commitment.

**See also**: [Shreyas Doshi Interview - Episode 3](/people/product/lenny-rachitsky/podcasts/2022-06-07-shreyas-doshi.md)

### 7. The Pre-Mortem Framework

**From Shreyas Doshi**

**When to use**: Before launching major initiatives or projects

**How it works**:
Imagine the project has already failed spectacularly. Then ask:
1. What went wrong?
2. What could we have done differently?
3. What signals should we have watched for?
4. How do we prevent this outcome?

**Benefits**:
- Uncovers risks early (before they're expensive)
- Creates psychological safety to surface concerns
- Builds mitigation plans proactively
- Reduces hindsight bias

**At Stripe**: Regular pre-mortem meetings became core to their culture, catching issues before launch.

**See also**: [Shreyas Doshi Interview - Episode 3](/people/product/lenny-rachitsky/podcasts/2022-06-07-shreyas-doshi.md)

### 8. The LNO Prioritization Framework

**From Shreyas Doshi**

**When to use**: Managing competing priorities across products or initiatives

**What it stands for**:
- **L**: Low-hanging fruit (quick wins, visible impact)
- **N**: New growth opportunities (innovation, differentiation)
- **O**: Optimization/iteration (improving existing value)

**How to use**:
1. Inventory all potential work
2. Categorize each as L, N, or O
3. Allocate resources across the three categories
4. Review balance periodically

**Insight**: Most product teams skew too heavily to O (optimization) and miss N (new growth opportunities that drive long-term success.

**See also**: [Shreyas Doshi Interview - Episode 3](/people/product/lenny-rachitsky/podcasts/2022-06-07-shreyas-doshi.md)

### 9. The Opportunity Cost Framework

**From Shreyas Doshi**

**When to use**: Planning and prioritization

**How it works**:
Instead of asking "What's the ROI of this project?", ask "What's the opportunity cost of NOT doing this?"

**Key distinction**:
- **ROI thinking**: Focuses on what you gain from doing X
- **Opportunity cost thinking**: Focuses on what you lose by NOT doing X

**Example**:
- ROI view: "Building this feature has 3x ROI"
- Opportunity cost view: "If we don't build this, we lose $2M ARR and 3 key customers"

**Why it matters**:
ROI thinking underestimates cost of inaction. Opportunity cost thinking reveals true cost of not solving problems.

### 10. The Decision Journal Framework

**When to use**: Building decision-making muscle and learning over time

**How it works**:
For every significant decision, record:
1. What was the decision?
2. What information did we have?
3. What options did we consider?
4. What was the decision?
5. What was our rationale?
6. What outcome did we observe?

**Review periodically**:
Every quarter, review past decisions and ask:
- What did we get right?
- What did we get wrong?
- What patterns emerge?
- What should we do differently?

**Power of compounding**: Small improvements in decision quality compound massively over time.

## Choosing the Right Framework

| Decision Type | Best Framework | Why |
|--------------|-----------------|------|
| Feature prioritization | Confidence Meter | Rates opportunities based on validation |
| Multi-stakeholder decision | 3-2-1 + Decision Owner | Options + Accountability |
| Risky new initiative | Pre-Mortem + Two-Door Test | Surface risks + Exit options |
| Endless debate | Time-Boxing | Force decision, keep moving |
| Strategic planning | LNO + Opportunity Cost | Balance types of work |
| Long-term learning | Decision Journal | Improve decision quality over time |

## Common Decision Pitfalls

### ❌ Analysis Paralysis
**Problem**: Overthinking and delaying decisions
**Fix**: Set time limits, use confidence meter, decide with 70% certainty

### ❌ False Consensus
**Problem**: Waiting for everyone to agree
**Fix**: Gather input, make clear decision, communicate rationale

### ❌ Anchoring on First Option
**Problem**: First idea gets undue weight
**Fix**: Use 3-2-1 framework, always consider multiple options

### ❌ Missing Stakeholder Input
**Problem**: Decision-maker decides in isolation
**Fix**: Use stakeholder alignment framework, gather input proactively

### ❌ Unclear Decision Criteria
**Problem**: Different people optimizing for different outcomes
**Fix**: Explicitly state success criteria before discussing options

### ❌ Not Documenting Rationale
**Problem**: Can't learn from decisions, can't communicate why
**Fix**: Decision journal, written rationale, visible decision record

## Decision-Making Culture Tips

### For Leaders
1. **Model decisiveness**: Make decisions clearly and explain your thinking
2. **Create safety**: Encourage pre-mortems and risk identification
3. **Own outcomes**: Take responsibility for decisions (not just credit for good ones)
4. **Learn publicly**: Share decision reviews, what worked, what didn't
5. **Trust your team**: Empower decision owners, don't second-guess

### For Teams
1. **Provide input proactively**: Don't wait to be asked
2. **Surface risks early**: Use pre-mortems, raise concerns
3. **Support decisions**: Once decided, align and execute
4. **Learn from outcomes**: Track what happened, share insights
5. **Propose frameworks**: Help team discover better decision processes

## Quick Reference

**Before any decision**:
- Who owns this decision?
- What options exist?
- What's the deadline?
- What criteria matter?
- Who needs input?
- What's our confidence?

**After any decision**:
- What did we decide?
- Why did we decide this?
- How will we track outcomes?
- How will we communicate it?
- When will we review?

**Continuous improvement**:
- Quarterly decision reviews
- Framework effectiveness checks
- Culture health assessments
- Process optimizations
