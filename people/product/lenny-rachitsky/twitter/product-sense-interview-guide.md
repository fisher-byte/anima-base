# The Definitive Guide to Mastering Product Sense Interviews

> Originally popularized by Lenny Rachitsky, this guide breaks down how to ace product sense interviews step by step.

## What is Product Sense?

**Product sense is compressed experience, not innate talent.**

It's the ability to:
- Identify what users will actually value
- Prioritize the right problems to solve
- Make good trade-offs between options
- Spot opportunities others miss

Good news: This is a learnable skill. Great news: You can demonstrate it systematically in interviews.

## How Product Sense Interviews Work

**The scenario**: You're given a product or feature question and asked how you'd approach it.

**What interviewers are evaluating**:
- Your thought process (not just the answer)
- Customer empathy
- Business understanding
- Prioritization skills
- Creative problem solving

**What they're NOT looking for**:
- The "right" answer (there often isn't one)
- Memorized frameworks
- Perfect recall of existing features
- Technical implementation details

## The Framework for Acing Product Sense Interviews

### Step 1: Clarify the Problem (2 minutes)

**Ask clarifying questions to set context:**

```
Examples:
- What's the goal we're optimizing for?
- Who is the target user?
- What constraints exist (time, budget, resources)?
- What's the timeline?
- What does success look like?
```

**Why this matters**: Shows you don't rush to solutions and understand the full problem space.

### Step 2: Understand the User (3 minutes)

**Build a mental model of the user:**

```
- Who are they? (demographics, tech-savviness)
- What's their job-to-be-done?
- What frustrates them now?
- What alternatives exist?
- When/where do they use this?
```

**Pro tip**: "Tell me about the last time you..." questions reveal real behaviors vs. stated preferences.

### Step 3: Identify the Core Problem (2 minutes)

**Don't jump to solutions - define the problem first:**

```
Framework:
1. What's the user trying to accomplish?
2. What's preventing them?
3. Why does this matter to them?
4. What's the business impact of solving this?
```

**Example**:
- Bad: "They need a better search"
- Good: "Users can't find what they need quickly enough, which increases churn and reduces engagement"

### Step 4: Brainstorm Solutions (5 minutes)

**Generate multiple options before committing:**

```
Approach:
- Think broadly first (don't self-censor)
- Include "obvious" and "innovative" ideas
- Consider different problem angles
- Aim for 3-5 distinct directions
```

**Categories to explore**:
- User experience improvements
- New features or capabilities
- Process/flow changes
- System/infrastructure changes
- Behavioral nudges

### Step 5: Evaluate & Prioritize (5 minutes)

**Use a simple framework to pick best direction:**

```
Evaluation criteria:
- User value (how much does this help?)
- Business impact (revenue, growth, retention)
- Technical complexity (feasibility, resources)
- Time to value (how fast can we ship?)
- Risk level (what could go wrong?)
```

**Prioritization method**: Rate each on 1-5 scale or use a 2x2 matrix (impact vs. effort).

### Step 6: Dive Deep on Top Choice (5 minutes)

**Flesh out the winning idea:**

```
Structure:
1. What specifically are we building?
2. How will it work? (user flow)
3. What's the MVP version?
4. How will we measure success?
5. What might go wrong? (risks)
```

**Show your thinking**: Explain trade-offs you're making and why this approach wins.

### Step 7: Measure & Iterate (3 minutes)

**Define how you'd validate and improve:**

```
Metrics:
- North Star: What's the primary measure?
- Leading indicators: What predicts success early?
- Guardrail metrics: What must we NOT hurt?
- Success criteria: What's good enough?

Learning loop:
- Ship fast → Measure → Learn → Iterate
```

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### ❌ Jumping to Solutions Without Understanding
**Problem**: Solving the wrong problem
**Fix**: Spend time clarifying the problem and user context

### ❌ Being Too Solution-Focused vs. Problem-Focused
**Problem**: Missing better problem definitions
**Fix**: Keep asking "why this matters" to get to core problem

### ❌ Over-indexing on Existing Features
**Problem**: Just copying competitors
**Fix**: Understand user needs, not just current implementations

### ❌ Ignoring Business Context
**Problem**: Great solution, wrong for the business
**Fix**: Consider company goals, resources, constraints

### ❌ Being Too Generic
**Problem**: Advice that applies anywhere applies nowhere
**Fix**: Be specific about the product, user, and context

## How to Practice & Improve

### Practice Method 1: Real-World Critique
Pick a feature you use daily and critique it:
- What problem is it solving?
- Is it the right problem?
- How would you improve it?
- What are the trade-offs?

### Practice Method 2: Interview Yourself
Record yourself answering product questions:
- Watch for jumping to solutions
- Count how many questions you ask
- Check if you define user clearly
- Time each section

### Practice Method 3: Reverse Engineer Successful Products
Study products you admire:
- What user problem do they solve?
- How did they approach it?
- What alternatives did they consider?
- What makes their approach special?

### Practice Method 4: Mock Interviews with Feedback
Partner with other PMs or mentors:
- Do timed practice sessions
- Ask for specific feedback
- Practice with different product types
- Vary the scenarios

## Example Walkthrough

**Question**: "How would you improve Airbnb's review system?"

**Strong response**:
1. **Clarify**: "What's the goal? Trust improvement? Review volume? Quality?"
2. **Understand user**: Guests want reliability, hosts want fair feedback, Airbnb wants trust
3. **Problem**: Reviews are sparse early, uneven, sometimes unhelpful
4. **Brainstorm**: Prompts, photos, timing, weighting, incentives, verification
5. **Prioritize**: Timed prompts (high impact, low effort)
6. **Deep dive**: Prompt within 24h, contextual questions, reminder system
7. **Measure**: Review completion rate, review quality, booking correlation

**Weak response**:
1. **Skips clarifying** - just starts suggesting features
2. **Generic ideas** - "Make it easier to leave reviews" (how?)
3. **No user empathy** - doesn't consider guests vs. hosts
4. **No trade-offs** - suggests everything at once
5. **No metrics** - no way to know if it worked

## What Interviewers Actually Want

They want to see:
✅ Clear structured thinking
✅ Customer-first mindset
✅ Comfort with ambiguity
✅ Ability to prioritize
✅ Self-awareness about trade-offs
✅ Communication skills

They DON'T want:
❌ The "correct" answer
❌ Memorized frameworks applied mechanically
❌ Technical implementation details
❌ Buzzwords without substance

## Key Takeaways

1. **Product sense is learnable** - it's pattern recognition from experience
2. **Structure matters** - clear thinking framework > clever answers
3. **Be problem-focused** - understand before you solve
4. **Show your work** - explain your reasoning, not just conclusions
5. **Prioritize explicitly** - make trade-offs visible
6. **Define success** - metrics show you're thinking about outcomes
7. **Stay humble** - acknowledge what you don't know
