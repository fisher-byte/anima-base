# Making of a Manager with Julie Zhuo (Let's Fix Work)

## Overview

Julie Zhuo, VP of Product Design at Facebook and author of "The Making of a Manager," discusses the difference between management and leadership, her three-bucket framework for daily management work, and why American culture struggles with feedback. Originally recorded for the Watermark Conference panel on "managing up."

- **When**: September 9, 2019
- **Host**: Laurie Ruettimann (workplace culture expert)
- **Show**: Let's Fix Work (later rebranded to Punk Rock HR)
- **Julie's Role**: VP of Product Design at Facebook
- **Book Launch**: Promoting "The Making of a Manager: What To Do When Everyone Looks to You"
- **Connection**: Met at Watermark Conference for Women in Silicon Valley

## Key Topics Discussed

### 1. What Inspired the Book

**The Gap**: 
- Most people become managers without formal training
- Corporate management training often feels disconnected from reality
- Wanted to share practical lessons from her journey
- Make management knowledge accessible to everyone

**Personal Story**:
- Started as Facebook's first intern in 2006
- Became manager without really knowing what that meant
- Learned through trial, error, and lots of mistakes
- Wanted to help others avoid her struggles

**Core Message**: "Management doesn't need to be overcomplicated"

### 2. Leadership vs Management: The Clear Distinction

**Management (A Role)**:
- It's a job with specific responsibilities
- Can be given to you by organization
- Can be taken away from you
- Core job: Help your team achieve great outcomes
- Requires particular skills and tools

**Leadership (A Quality)**:
- Not a role - it's a trait or characteristic
- Must be earned through behavior
- Can't be given or taken away
- Someone others want to follow
- Takes initiative and rallies people
- Can exist at any level (manager or IC)

**The Intersection**: 
"For managers to be effective, they should exhibit qualities of great leadership. But you can be a leader without being a manager."

**Why This Matters**:
- Clarifies what you're responsible for as a manager
- Helps ICs understand they can still lead
- Explains why some managers aren't effective leaders
- Shows leadership development is separate from role advancement

### 3. The Three Management Buckets (Daily Focus Areas)

#### Bucket 1: PEOPLE

**Key Activities**:
- **Hiring & Recruiting**: Bringing right people onto team
- **Coaching**: Amplifying each person's strengths
- **Performance Management**: Giving feedback, adjusting behavior
- **Development**: Growing people's careers
- **Team Composition**: Right mix of skills and personalities

**Most Important**: "Ensuring you're bringing the right people onto the team"

**Why It Matters**: Everything else breaks if you don't have the right people

#### Bucket 2: PROCESS

**The Orchestra Analogy**: "If you have an orchestra and you've got the right violinists and you've got the right percussion players; you still need to make sure that everyone knows how to work together and how to play music together."

**Key Activities**:
- **Coordination**: How team works together
- **Communication Rhythms**: Meetings, updates, check-ins
- **Conflict Resolution**: Systems for handling disagreements
- **Decision-Making**: Who decides what, and how
- **Workflows**: How work moves through the team

**Common Mistake**: Assuming good people automatically work well together

**Right Processes Include**:
- Everyone knows their role
- Clear communication channels
- No one blocking each other's work
- Efficient decision-making frameworks

#### Bucket 3: PURPOSE

**The Sheet Music Metaphor**: "Making sure that every single one of the people on your team has the same understanding of what success looks like... it's ensuring that everyone has the same sheet music."

**Why Purpose Matters**:
- Prevents conflicts from misalignment
- Becomes MORE critical as team grows
- With 10 people, easy to stay aligned
- With 100+ people, slight misalignment = major problems

**What Purpose Includes**:
- Clear goals and metrics
- Shared understanding of priorities
- Common vision of success
- Context for why we're doing this work

**Scaling Challenge**: "Once you have dozens of people, hundreds or thousands, who all have a slightly different perspective of what success looks like; they're not going to be working together as efficiently as they could be."

### 4. The Manager as Therapist Phenomenon

**The Blurred Lines**:
- Modern managers often expected to be therapists
- People bring personal problems to managers
- Line between professional and personal support unclear

**Julie's Perspective**:
- Managers should care about people as humans
- But there are boundaries
- Not trained therapists or counselors
- Can direct people to proper resources

**The Balance**:
- Be empathetic and supportive
- Recognize when issues are beyond your scope
- Know when to involve HR or employee assistance
- Maintain professional boundaries while being human

### 5. When Julie Moved from Manager to Leader

**The Turning Point**:
- Not a single moment, but gradual realization
- Started when others outside her team sought her input
- People wanted to follow her vision, not just take direction
- Influence extended beyond formal authority

**Signs of Leader Emergence**:
- Others asking for advice
- People adopting your approaches voluntarily
- Influence beyond your direct reports
- Others wanting to work with you

**Key Insight**: You can't declare yourself a leader. Others recognize it through your actions.

### 6. Feedback: America's Cultural Problem

**The Issue**: "Our culture doesn't know how to give feedback"

**Why Americans Struggle**:
- Conflict-avoidant culture
- Confuse kindness with avoiding hard truths
- Afraid of hurting feelings
- Don't practice giving direct feedback

**The Cost**:
- People don't know where they stand
- Small issues become big problems
- Talent doesn't develop optimally
- Team performance suffers

**What Great Feedback Looks Like**:

**Definition**: "Great feedback is simply feedback that changes you in a way that later on you're grateful for and that you believe made your life better."

**Characteristics**:
1. **Specific**: Not vague generalities
2. **Timely**: Given soon after observation
3. **Inspiring**: Makes you want to change
4. **Balanced**: Recognizes both strengths and areas to improve
5. **Actionable**: Clear what to do differently

**Common Misconception**: "We think we should only give feedback if something isn't working or if something is broken"

**Reality**: "Feedback can be somebody recognizing that you are really great at this thing" - recognizing strengths is also powerful feedback

### 7. Do You Need Formal Training to Be a Good Manager?

**Julie's Answer**: NO

**Why Not**:
- Management is primarily learned through doing
- Books and training help, but practice is essential
- Every team and situation is different
- Real learning comes from mistakes and adjustments

**How to Learn Without Training**:
1. **Read thoughtfully**: Books like hers provide frameworks
2. **Learn from others**: Observe great managers
3. **Practice**: Try things, see what works
4. **Reflect**: Think about what worked and why
5. **Get feedback**: Ask your team how you're doing

**The Book's Purpose**: 
"How can this book be helpful for professionals who don't have access to management or leadership training?"
- Provides frameworks anyone can apply
- Shares real examples from her journey
- Makes implicit knowledge explicit
- Accessible to anyone wanting to improve

## Notable Quotes

> "Management is a role; it's a job. Leadership is not a role. Leadership is a quality."

> "If you have talented people and they know how to work together, but they don't have the same picture of what success looks like, you're still going to get conflict."

> "Great feedback is simply feedback that changes you in a way that later on you're grateful for."

> "Oftentimes, [management training] includes hiring and recruiting. It's thinking about how to coach the current people on your team and ensuring that their strengths are amplified across the team."

> "Once you have dozens of people, hundreds or thousands, who all have a slightly different perspective of what success looks like; they're not going to be working together as efficiently as they could be."

## Practical Takeaways

1. **Check your three buckets daily**: People, Process, Purpose - are you neglecting any area?
2. **Clarify the management/leadership distinction**: Helps you know what to develop
3. **Make feedback a gift**: Frame it as helping people grow, not criticism
4. **Don't wait for training**: Start managing well now using available resources
5. **Purpose scales with size**: The bigger your team, the more critical shared understanding becomes
6. **Balance kindness and honesty**: Being supportive doesn't mean avoiding hard truths
