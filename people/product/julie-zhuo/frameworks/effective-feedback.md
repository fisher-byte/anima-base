# Effective Feedback Framework

> "Task-specific feedback is the most effective way to help someone improve." — Julie Zhuo

## Overview

Julie Zhuo's feedback framework focuses on making feedback **specific, actionable, and timely**. She argues that vague feedback like "This needs more impact" is useless because it provides no concrete guidance.

## The Framework

### Three Components of Effective Feedback

```
┌─────────────────┐    ┌─────────────────┐    ┌─────────────────┐
│   1. CONCRETE   │ → │  2. CLEAR       │ → │  3. ACTIONABLE  │
│   What happened │    │  What success   │    │  Next steps     │
│   specifically  │    │  looks like     │    │  to take        │
└─────────────────┘    └─────────────────┘    └─────────────────┘
```

### 1. Be Concrete 📍

**What it means**: Describe specific behaviors, examples, and situations.

**Instead of**: "You need to communicate better"

**Say**: "In yesterday's meeting, when asked about project status, your answer was unclear. The stakeholders didn't know if we were on track."

**Questions to ask yourself**:
- Can I point to a specific instance?
- Would someone else recognize the behavior I'm describing?
- Am I describing observable actions, not assumptions about intent?

### 2. Clarify What Success Looks Like 🎯

**What it means**: Paint a clear picture of the expected outcome.

**Instead of**: "This design needs more impact"

**Say**: "The design should immediately communicate our value proposition. Users should understand what we do within 3 seconds of landing on the page."

**Questions to ask yourself**:
- Does the person know what "good" looks like?
- Can they measure whether they've succeeded?
- Have I shown, not just told, what I'm looking for?

### 3. Suggest Next Steps 🚀

**What it means**: Provide actionable guidance on how to improve.

**Instead of**: "Work on your presentation skills"

**Say**: "For your next presentation, try structuring it with a clear 'Problem → Solution → Ask' format. I'd recommend practicing with a colleague first and timing yourself to stay under 10 minutes."

**Questions to ask yourself**:
- Have I given at least one concrete action they can take?
- Is the action within their control?
- Can they start immediately?

## Feedback Types

### Task-Specific Feedback (Most Effective)

Given immediately after specific work or behavior:

| Situation | Poor Feedback | Effective Feedback |
|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| Code review | "This is messy" | "The function starting at line 42 does 5 things. Could you break it into smaller, single-purpose functions?" |
| Presentation | "Good job" | "Your opening story about the customer really grabbed attention. The data section felt rushed—try cutting 2 slides." |
| Meeting behavior | "You talk too much" | "In today's meeting, you spoke for 15 of the 30 minutes. Try asking 2-3 questions before sharing your view next time." |

### Behavioral Feedback (For Patterns)

Given when you notice recurring patterns:

**Structure**:
1. "I've noticed [pattern] in the past few [timeframe]"
2. "Here are specific examples: [concrete instances]"
3. "The impact is [effect on team/work]"
4. "I'd like to see [desired behavior] instead"

### Strengths-Based Feedback

Often overlooked but equally important:

> "The best managers don't just correct problems—they help people recognize and double down on their strengths."

**Structure**:
1. "You're particularly good at [specific strength]"
2. "For example, [concrete instance]"
3. "This helps the team by [impact]"
4. "How can we create more opportunities for you to use this?"

## Timing Matters

### When to Give Feedback

| Timing | Best For |
|--------|----------|
| **Immediately** | Task-specific feedback on work products |
| **Same day** | Behavioral feedback from meetings/interactions |
| **1:1 meetings** | Patterns, growth areas, career development |
| **Scheduled reviews** | Comprehensive performance discussions |

### The 24-Hour Rule

Julie recommends giving feedback within 24 hours when possible:
- Details are fresh
- Context is clear
- Recipient can immediately act on it

## Creating a Feedback Culture

### As a Manager

1. **Ask for feedback first**: Model vulnerability by requesting feedback on your own work
2. **Make it routine**: Regular feedback normalizes the practice
3. **Celebrate improvement**: Acknowledge when feedback is applied

### As a Receiver

1. **Assume good intent**: The person is trying to help you improve
2. **Ask clarifying questions**: "Can you give me an example?"
3. **Thank them**: Even if you disagree, appreciate the effort

## Common Mistakes

❌ **Sandwich feedback** — Hiding criticism between compliments dilutes the message

❌ **Waiting for reviews** — Annual feedback is too late to be useful

❌ **Making it personal** — "You are X" vs. "Your work on X showed Y"

❌ **Feedback without context** — "You need to improve X" without explaining why it matters
