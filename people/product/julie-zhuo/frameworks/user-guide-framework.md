# User Guide to Working With You

> "What if every person you worked with came with a user guide?" — Julie Zhuo

## Overview

Julie Zhuo's "User Guide" framework is a self-reflection tool that helps individuals articulate their working style, preferences, and quirks to teammates. Originally published in her *Looking Glass* newsletter, it's designed to accelerate trust-building and collaboration.

## The Framework

### Why Create a User Guide?

- **Accelerates onboarding**: New teammates learn how to work with you faster
- **Prevents misunderstandings**: Explicit expectations reduce friction
- **Builds self-awareness**: Writing it forces reflection on your patterns
- **Models vulnerability**: Sharing quirks normalizes imperfection

### The Template

#### 1. My Style

Describe your general approach to work:

**Questions to answer**:
- How do I like to communicate? (Slack vs. meetings vs. documents)
- What's my decision-making style? (Consensus vs. decisive)
- How do I handle conflict?
- Am I more structured or flexible?

**Example** (Julie's own):
> "I tend to listen more than talk in meetings... After the meeting, I'll often reflect on how I actually feel about something and will follow up with people asynchronously."

#### 2. What I Value

What matters most to you in how work gets done:

**Questions to answer**:
- What behaviors do I respect most in colleagues?
- What frustrates me?
- What do I prioritize when making trade-offs?

**Example**:
> "I value directness. If something is on your mind, please just say it. I'd rather have an uncomfortable conversation than wonder what you're really thinking."

#### 3. What I Don't Have Patience For

Be honest about your triggers:

**Questions to answer**:
- What behaviors frustrate me?
- What feels like a waste of time?
- When do I get cranky?

**Example** (Julie's own):
> "I have low tolerance for not thinking through the problem... Often what's implied but not said is far more consequential than what's been written."

#### 4. How to Best Communicate with Me

Tactical guidance on getting your attention:

**Questions to answer**:
- Preferred channel for different message types?
- How much context do I need?
- When is the best time to reach me?
- How should bad news be delivered?

**Example**:
> "For quick questions: Slack. For complex topics: Schedule a meeting or send a document for async review. I'm most responsive in mornings."

#### 5. How to Help Me

What support looks like for you:

**Questions to answer**:
- What do I struggle with?
- How can others help me be more effective?
- What kind of feedback do I need more of?

**Example** (Julie's own):
> "I often need time to process before I can articulate what I think. Give me space to follow up later rather than expecting an immediate answer."

#### 6. What People Misunderstand About Me

Address common misconceptions:

**Questions to answer**:
- What do people often assume incorrectly?
- What about my style gets misread?
- What do I want people to know?

**Example**:
> "My quietness isn't disengagement—I'm processing. When I don't speak up immediately, it doesn't mean I disagree."

## Creating Your User Guide

### Step 1: Self-Reflection

Spend 30 minutes answering these questions:
- What do colleagues consistently get wrong about me?
- What behaviors bring out my best work?
- What are my "hot buttons" that trigger negative reactions?
- How have I changed my style over the years?

### Step 2: Get Input

Ask 2-3 trusted colleagues:
- "What should people know about working with me?"
- "What's something about my style that took you a while to figure out?"
- "When have you seen me at my best/worst?"

### Step 3: Write and Share

- Keep it 1-2 pages max
- Use specific examples, not vague generalities
- Update annually as you evolve
- Share with new teammates proactively

## Example Structure

```markdown
# Working with [Your Name]

## My Style
[2-3 sentences on your general approach]

## What I Value
[3-5 bullet points]

## What I Don't Have Patience For
[Be honest—2-3 things]

## How to Communicate with Me
[Channel preferences, timing, context needs]

## How to Help Me
[What support looks like]

## What People Misunderstand About Me
[1-2 common misconceptions]
```

## Team Exercise

Julie suggests teams can do this together:

1. Everyone writes their own guide (20 min)
2. Share in small groups (2-3 people per group)
3. Ask clarifying questions
4. Post guides in shared team space

**Outcome**: Faster trust-building and fewer miscommunications
