# Managing Your Manager Framework

> "Most of us do a shitty job of getting the most out of our manager." — Julie Zhuo

## Overview

This framework from Julie Zhuo's *Looking Glass* newsletter addresses one of the most important but underutilized work relationships. It provides actionable strategies for making the manager-report relationship more productive—for both parties.

## Core Insight

Your manager is a **resource**, not just an authority figure. The relationship should be mutually beneficial:

| Your Manager Can Help You | You Help Your Manager By |
|---------------------------|--------------------------|
| Career guidance | Delivering results |
| Political navigation | Reducing their cognitive load |
| Resource allocation | Providing clear information |
| Skill development | Making them look good |
| Exposure to leaders | Flagging issues early |

## The Framework

### 1. Understand What Your Manager Actually Does

**Before you can manage up, understand their world**:

- What are their top 3 priorities this quarter?
- Who do they report to, and what pressures come from above?
- What problems keep them up at night?
- How is their performance measured?

**Action**: Ask directly in your next 1:1:
> "What are the most important things for you to accomplish this quarter? How can I help?"

### 2. Make Their Job Easier

**Reduce cognitive load**:

| Instead of This | Do This |
|-----------------|---------|
| "I have a problem" | "I have a problem. Here are 2-3 options. I recommend X because..." |
| "What should I do?" | "Here's what I'm planning. Any concerns?" |
| "It's complicated" | "The key issue is X. Here's a one-page summary." |
| Updates in meetings | Brief written updates before meetings |

**The goal**: Your manager should feel *less* stressed after talking to you, not more.

### 3. Communicate Proactively

**Don't wait to be asked**:

```
Good cadence:
├── Weekly: Brief status update (wins, blockers, asks)
├── Monthly: Progress against goals
├── Quarterly: Career check-in
└── Always: Bad news travels immediately
```

**Bad news rule**: Your manager should never be surprised by something they should have known. Better to over-communicate than under-communicate.

### 4. Ask for What You Need

**Your manager can't read minds**:

| Need | How to Ask |
|------|------------|
| More autonomy | "I'd like to own X end-to-end. Here's my plan for how I'd approach it." |
| More feedback | "I'd love more specific feedback on X. What's working and what isn't?" |
| Career growth | "I'm interested in developing [skill]. Any opportunities you see?" |
| More context | "It would help me to understand the 'why' behind this decision." |

**Key insight**: Asking for help isn't weakness—it's how you get better and how your manager succeeds.

### 5. Give Your Manager Feedback

**Yes, upward feedback**:

Most managers get surprisingly little feedback from their reports. Thoughtful upward feedback can dramatically improve your relationship.

**Structure**:
1. Ask permission: "Would it be helpful if I shared feedback on our working relationship?"
2. Be specific: Point to concrete examples
3. Focus on impact: "When X happens, it affects my work by Y"
4. Suggest alternatives: "It would help me if..."

**Example**:
> "I've noticed that when priorities shift frequently, I have trouble making progress. It would help me if we could batch changes to once a week, or at least signal when something is truly urgent vs. nice-to-have."

### 6. Align on Expectations

**Close the expectations gap**:

In every 1:1 or project kickoff, explicitly clarify:
- **What does success look like?** (Be specific)
- **What's the timeline?** (And what flexibility exists)
- **How will you communicate progress?** (Format, frequency)
- **What decisions can you make vs. need approval?** (Autonomy boundaries)

**Document it**: After conversations, send a brief recap email: "Just to confirm, we agreed that..."

## The 1:1 Meeting as a Tool

Julie views the 1:1 as **your meeting**, not your manager's:

### Come Prepared

**Your agenda, not theirs**:
- What do I need from this conversation?
- What do they need to know?
- What decisions need to be made?
- What feedback do I want to give or receive?

### Use the Time Well

| Waste of 1:1 | Good Use of 1:1 |
|--------------|-----------------|
| Status updates (do async) | Career conversations |
| Asking questions you could Google | Navigating difficult situations |
| Venting without purpose | Processing feedback |
| No agenda | Strategic discussions |

## When the Relationship Isn't Working

### Signs of Trouble

- You dread talking to your manager
- You feel micromanaged or ignored
- You're consistently misaligned on priorities
- Feedback is rare, vague, or only negative

### What to Do

1. **Address it directly**: Have an honest conversation about what's not working
2. **Propose solutions**: Don't just complain—suggest how to improve
3. **Give it time**: Change takes effort from both sides
4. **Know when to move on**: Some relationships can't be fixed

> "Stop tolerating mediocre management. You have more agency than you think."

## Summary Checklist

✅ Understand your manager's priorities and pressures
✅ Come with solutions, not just problems
✅ Communicate proactively—especially bad news
✅ Ask explicitly for what you need
✅ Give thoughtful upward feedback
✅ Align expectations and document agreements
✅ Own your 1:1 agenda
